 executive summary
the transition to postquantum cryptography pqc will not
necessarily be quick or inexpensive however ignoring the
issue will likely lead to more costly missteps and increase
the risk of overlooking potential issues resulting in greater
time and financial losses implementing a companywide
program to investigate internal security issues related to
quantum computing and to create plans to address them
provides the best opportunity for reducing migration
expenses creating an environment secure against
quantum attack reducing the likelihood of missed vulnerabilities and providing the opportunity to correct long
standing problems while the scope of the work may be daunting the financial industry has faced similar challenges
in the past with other cryptographic migrations such as des to des sha to sha and des to aes
the fundamental difficulty in achieving an effective migration to pqc is the scale and effort required across multiple
industry sectors systems and networks put another way the cryptographic migration task is a complex problem
involving change at multiple levels in addition to these complexities the timeline for implementation must also be
considered
like any complex or challenging problem simplification is a good way to start the problem can be more easily
managed by breaking it down into a prioritized sequence of individual steps however in terms of the pqc migration
building such a list involves understanding the requirements of the standards community vendors and endusers
this document and others being produced by x will aid organizations preparing to migrate to pqc for more
information on related documents please visit the x website wwwxorg or contact x adminxorg

pqc is a branch of cryptography concerned with asymmetric cryptographic systems resistant to attacks which utilize
either quantum computers or classical computers standards bodies like asc x are working with organizations like
the national institute of standards and technology nist to select secure pqc algorithms and incorporate them
into our financial standards that said the effort to standardize a suite of pqc algorithms is still a work in progress
with an initial set of postquantumalgorithms expected by late  in  nist has released their first batch of
three pqc algorithms more details on these algorithms can be found in annex a of this document nist has started
work on the next round of pqc candidates
the migration to pqc will by necessity require significant work in many areas such as
 educating personnel on the issues related to how quantum computers can break cryptography
 creating a cryptographic asset inventory to identify all the cryptographic systems in use
 prioritize current systems for remediation based on a risk assessment
 developing and implementing a plan to migrate to pqc solutions where required
 working with vendors to develop and deploy pqc solutions in third party products
 using the pqc migration as an opportunity to investigate and incorporate features of an agile
architecture where appropriate
companies and government agencies are facing a critical challenge the need to migrate to postquantum
cryptography in order to protect sensitive data and networks from attacks by quantum computers
this paper provides guidance for the financial industry government agencies and other organization to help them
prepare for the migration to pqc in addition this paper also identifies the challenges that companies and agencies
will face in migrating to pqc these include
 the need to educate personnel on quantum computing issues and the new pqc algorithms
 there may be a need to select between multiple pqc algorithms for a given use case
 the cost of developing and deploying pqc solutions
 the need to coordinate the migration across multiple organizations and networks

 data value and lifecycle
when prioritizing cryptographic assets in the financial industry for protection
against quantum attacks it is essential to consider the value and criticality of the
data they safeguard the financial industry deals with a vast amount of sensitive
information and not all data elements have the same level of importance
 determining data value
here are some steps to determine the value of data that needs protection from a
quantum attack
 data classification classify the data based on its sensitivity or importance categorize data into levels
such as public internal confidential personally identifiable information pii financial transactions
cryptographic keys and other critical assets
 financial impact analysis assess the potential financial impact of a data breach or compromise for
each category consider losses liabilities regulatory fines and the erosion of customer trust
 regulatory compliance consider regulatory requirements specific to the financial industry identify
data elements governed by regulations such as pci dss gdpr glba or local financial data
protection laws prioritize protection for data that must comply with these regulations
 business continuity dependence evaluate how the data supports business continuity and the
smooth operation of financial systems prioritize data that is crucial for uninterrupted business
processes
 trade secrets and intellectual property identify data that constitutes trade secrets intellectual
property or proprietary financial models such data is critical to maintaining a competitive edge and
should be given high priority for protection
 data usage and access analyze the frequency of data usage and the number of users who have
access to each data category data that is frequently accessed or widely distributed may require
stronger protection measures
 interconnectedness and dependencies understand how different data elements are interconnected
and their dependencies on one another prioritize protecting data that could serve as a gateway to
accessing other sensitive information

 quantum vulnerability evaluate the susceptibility of different data elements to quantum attacks data
vulnerable to harvest now decrypt later attacks should be prioritized for quantumsafe protection
 customer and stakeholder trust consider the impact of a data breach on customer and stakeholder
trust protecting data that directly affects client trust is crucial for maintaining a positive reputation
 costbenefit analysis conduct a costbenefit analysis to evaluate the resources needed to implement
quantumsafe protection for each data category balance the potential impact of a quantum attack
against the investment needed for protection
by following these steps financial institutions can effectively prioritize inventory items for protection from a quantum
attack the goal is to focus resources on securing the most critical and sensitive data ensuring resilience against
emerging quantum computing threats while complying with regulatory requirements and maintaining the trust of
customers and stakeholders
 how long does the data require protection
the required protection lifespan of data significantly influences an
organizations pqc migration strategy sensitive data requiring
longterm protection is more vulnerable to harvest now decrypt
later attacks than shortlived data and should therefore be
prioritized in the postquantum migration process the duration for
which data must be protected depends on a variety of legal
regulatory business and security factors key factors influencing
data protection timelines include
 data retention policies organizations must adhere to
data retention policies mandated by laws and regulations
specific to their industry these policies outline the minimum and maximum duration for which certain types
of data must be retained for compliance purposes
 data classification different data categories have varying retention requirements based on their sensitivity
and criticality highly sensitive data may require longer protection periods compared to less critical
information
 legal and regulatory requirements laws and regulations may specify specific retention periods for
different types of data for example financial institutions may need to retain transaction records for a certain
number of years to comply with financial reporting standards
 contractual obligations agreements with customers partners or service providers may specify how long
certain data must be retained and protected
 statute of limitations in legal matters the statute of limitations determines the maximum period during
which legal actions can be initiated based on specific events data related to potential legal disputes may
need to be retained until the statute of limitations expires
 business necessity some data may be required for ongoing business operations or historical analysis
organizations may retain data for as long as it is deemed necessary to support business activities or
decisionmaking processes
 security considerations data sensitivity and exposure risk often determine how long protection must be
maintained highly sensitive data typically requires extended retention
 data lifecycle management data lifecycle management practices may influence the retention period
organizations may determine specific stages for data retention archival and disposal based on the datas
age and relevance
 customer consent retention timelines may be governed by customer consent agreements which can
define how long data may be stored

as part of an organizations cryptographic readiness
analysis it is important to establish policies and procedures
for conducting an organizationwide cryptographic audit and
inventory
many organizations are now facing the reality that they lack
a clear and comprehensive understanding of their
cryptographic footprintboth for data in motion and data at
rest
the key components of an organizational wide cryptographic audit and inventory include
 cryptographic inventory including

 cryptographic risk assessment
this challenge is compounded by the reliance on opensource and thirdparty software and systems and by the
technical debt accumulated from decades of complex hardware software and cloud infrastructure
as organizations adopt zero trust environments across converging it ot and iot systemsincluding on
premises offpremises and cloudbased deploymentsthey must develop a comprehensive understanding of their
cryptographic footprint the criticality of gaining a firm understanding of the entire cryptographic footprint is not only
desired but required to properly safeguard organizations both today and for the future
this problem is faced by all organizations globally and will be a critical hurdle that will need to be addressed as
organizations go through their pqc migration process it is important to note that like most audits this will be a
lengthy expensive and timeconsuming processand not one that can be easily addressed through ai or
automation alone
audits especially when performed for the first time are extremely labor intensive and require a wide array of skills
and a large team to do it properly such that the results are both useful and exhaustive
despite the uncertainty about when a cryptographically relevant quantum computer will be able to break current
encryption standards properly inventorying all cryptographic assets in use is the essential first step in pqc migration
preparednessand it is a today problem that financial institutions must begin addressing regardless of how they
plan to approach the migration

the reality is that an organization cannot begin planning a large digital transformation projectsuch as a pqc
migrationwithout first understanding the cryptographic assets currently in use across the enterprise
through the adoption of best practices and policies and procedures around creating an organizational wide crypto
system of record and beginning to implement cboms organizations can properly begin to discover inventory

 cryptographic inventory
a critical component of cryptographic lifecycle management is maintaining an uptodate cryptographic product
inventory such an inventory should include information about
 software products related to cryptography procured or developed inhouse
 vendors that provide a cryptographic product or service
 algorithms used including name purpose and parameters used to operate
 the location of static cryptographic keys both private and public
 internal or thirdparty organization responsible for deployment and maintenance
 protocols being used including configurations and
 any information relevant to the management of cryptography within the organization
by creating a cryptographic asset and product inventoryalong with a list of the organizations most sensitive and
critical data assets requiring extended securityorganizations can conduct more effective risk analysis this
approach helps us identify vulnerable data systems and processes safeguarding them against potential breaches
the level of effort required to fully inventory and understand an organizations vulnerability to a quantumenabled
threat actor is a function of the size and complexity of its enterprise network and computing assets this level of
understanding can support not only the implementation of pqc but also the broader management and operation of
the organizations security infrastructure other benefits include recovery from disasters security compromises as
well as facilitate high availability
as organizations conduct enterprisewide cryptographic audits they should also generate cryptography bills of
materials cbomsstructured inventories that catalog the cryptographic components in a system including
algorithms key types libraries protocols and usage contexts for legacy systems legacy systems are often the
largest contributors to cryptographic technical debt these systems frequently rely on outdated algorithms
hardcoded keys or undocumented libraries making them difficult to assess and remediate without visibility creating
legacy cboms enables targeted remediation of these weaknesses and supports more accurate tracking of inherited
technical debt in addition organizations may choose to share cbom data with vendors or partners using similar
legacy systemspromoting a coordinated approach to reducing technical debt and strengthening the overall
security posture across shared environments

 digital walletvault
some employees may be authorized to store or transport sensitive information such as cryptographic keys access
codes financial account data identification information and more this information may be stored in portable
devices know as a digital wallet or sometimes a digital vault the information stored on the device can be transferred
to a receiving device for example by nearfield communication nfc bluetooth wifi or cellular technologies
these devices generally rely on cryptography to securely store and transmit data many software and device vendors
provide these types of devices
some it administrators and field service personnel may use a digital wallet to store cryptographic information critical
to your it infrastructure such as fido passkeys therefore these devices must be considered in a plan to upgrade
to pqc transitioning these devices to pqc algorithms will likely require a vendorprovided upgrade or replacement
along with reloading credentials or data encrypted using pqcbased keys
note some devices that run a digital wallet may have insufficient computing power storage or other capabilities
needed to support pqc thereby necessitating an upgrade or replacement of the device
 password safe or key safe software
this software is similar to a digital vault but has a more limited purpose it is designed to store account names along
with their associated passwords and keys used for software or network functions this software is used by it
personnel to gain access or to update software that is part of your it infrastructure this type of product must be
included on any list of cryptographic assets to be reviewed for pqc support
password vaults are software tools to safely store user passwords basically to avoid users writing passwords on
sticky notes users might have different passwords for each application different passwords for each role to enforce

 dependencies between different cryptographic systems and
components for example in some cases master keys must be
migrated to pqc before subkeys
 the order in which different pqc systems and components should be

approaches for this consideration
 dependency graph audit cryptographic systems and create a dependency graph based on the
relationships identified a dependency graph visually represents the relationships between systems and
components it helps identify which elements must be migrated to pqc first and which can follow
 riskbased approach identify the systems and components that are most at risk from quantum attacks
and migrating those systems and components first note upstream or downstream components may need
to be migrated beforeor concurrently withthe main component
it may be beneficial to first migrate a lowervalue smaller project to gain insight and experience
after identifying dependencies and determining the order of implementation organizations should develop a
migration plan that outlines the steps required to transition each system and component to pqc the plan should
also include a timeline for the migration and a budget
here are some specific examples of dependencies and order of implementation considerations for pqc migration
 master keys master keys should be migrated to pqc first this is because the master keys are used to
generate and manage the subkeys
 subkeys subkeys can be migrated to pqc after the master keys have been migrated
 cryptographic protocols cryptographic protocols should be migrated to pqc in a coordinated manner
this will help to ensure that all systems and components are using the same pqc algorithms and protocols
see section titled standards polices and procedures for more details
 systems and applications systems and applications should be migrated to pqc in a way that minimizes
disruption this may require migrating some systems and applications first and others later
it is important to note that there is no onesizefitsall approach to determining dependencies and order of
implementation for pqc migration the specific dependencies and order of implementation will vary depending on
the organizations specific needs and environment
organizations should carefully consider the dependencies and order of implementation when migrating to pqc by
doing so they can minimize disruption and ensure a smooth transition

 pure quantumsafe certificates
the simplest approach to creating a quantumsafe certificate is to use a quantumsafe signature algorithm in place
of rsa or ecc algorithm oids are available for mlkem mldsa and slhdsa on the nist website
once the new algorithms are available in a crypto library it is relatively straightforward to include them into a
certificate in the standard way the ietf lamps draft draftietflampsdilithiumcertificates describes how this will
likely work for mldsa the ietf lamps draft draftietflampskybercertificates describes how this will likely work
for mlkem both drafts are proceeding through the ietf publication process
if the quantumsafe component or its implementation fails unexpectedly systems using such certificates could
become vulnerable
 composite quantumsafe certificates
a composite quantumsafe certificate contains a single key made up of two components a classical key and a
quantumsafe key it is referred to as a composite key because it combines the two parts typically by concatenating
them standards in this area are still being developed but they are maturing rapidly when signing with a composite
key signatures are generated with each key individually and then concatenated this produces a composite
signature verification is similarly pairwise the advantage is that in the event of an implementation flaw in the
quantumsafe algorithm the classical algorithm still provides protection against nonquantum attacks
note that each individual signature is also independently valid the security risks of this need to be carefully
considered in cases where that matters for secure operation both components of the signature need to be verified
and the verification must fail if either verification fails

 compliance assurance
vendor affirmations that a product process or service complies with a given
standardor meets other stated requirementsare typically insufficient if a product
claims to achieve certain properties and a user assumes the claim to be true then
there can be significant risk to that user if in reality the claim is false in particular
the risks associated with unverified or uncertified cryptography can be especially
significant therefore to validate vendor claims and reduce user risk products
should undergo objective formal verification and certification to ensure the
requirements are met
often for mature technologies there are corresponding mature standards and
certification and validation processes which vendors and users can rely on
unfortunately for nascent technologies there simply has not been enough time for
the relevant standards and certification and verification processes to develop this
is especially true for postquantum cryptography products
to avoid subtle misconceptions the following definitions can be helpful
 evaluation the process of evaluating the validity of the request that is presented
 verification confirms that the design is capable of meeting all stated requirements
 validation provides objective evidence that the product meets those requirements
 certification formal recognition that the product has successfully passed validation
there is often some confusion between verification and validation roughly speaking verification is something of a
sanitycheck it is a process to ensure that the system is designed in such a way to achieve all stated requirements
even if the system has not actually been built yet the goal is to verify the designs suitabilitythat is to answer the
question will the product meet the requirements

validation occurs after the product has been built when the product is validated it is objectively tested and
investigated to see whether the stated requirements have been met the goal is to validate the claims of
compliancethat is to determine does the product meet the requirements
certification is nothing more than a formalization of the compliance claims certification is often compared to earning
a diploma or academic degree it is a formal statement from the relevant authority eg the college or university
that the requirements of the program have been achieved at the end of the program an authority reviews that all
degree requirements have been met validation and if so issues the degree certification
it can also be helpful to draw a distinction between the terms compliance and assurance
 compliance demonstrated ability to meet ie conform to stated requirements
 assurance pertains to the level of trust and confidence in the quality of the product eg the security level
provided
some standards provide different levels of assurance where a higher assurance level instils higher confidence in
the security properties of the product for example the seven evaluation assurance levels eals of the common
criteria cc cryptographic module validation program cmvp provides four different levels which cryptographic
hardware security modules hsms can be validated against to be clear a higher level of assurance is not simply
the result of looking more closely at the product rather it is the result of compliance validation and certification of
the product against a more stringent set of requirements the more stringent the requirements the higher the
resulting assurance level

annex a  nistapproved postquantum cryptographic algorithms
on august   nist released the first three cryptographic algorithms approved as quantumsafe or resistant
a federal information processing standard fips was published for each a summary of the algorithms follows
the first set of nist algorithms consists of
 fips  modulelatticebased key encapsulation mechanism mlkem derived from crystals
kyber this generalpurpose algorithm allows two parties to establish a shared secret resistant to quantum
attacks that secret can then be used to encrypt data and ensure integrity mlkem is based on structured
lattices and is known for its speed and efficiency
 fips  modulelatticebased digital signature algorithm mldsa derived from crystalsdilithium
this latticebased signature algorithm is designed for general use offering relatively small keys and
signatures and fast performance in key generation signing and verification
 fips  stateless hashbased digital signature algorithm slhdsa derived from sphincs this
stateless hashbased signature scheme is highly robust but may be less practical for some applications due
to slower signing speeds and larger signature sizes
